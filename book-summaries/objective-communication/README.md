# Objective Communication: Writing, Speaking, and Arguing

**Author:** Leonard Peikoff (edited by Barry Wood)

**Source:** A course on effective communication based on Objectivist epistemology, delivered by Leonard Peikoff and edited from transcripts by Barry Wood.

## Overview

This book teaches that effective communication is a learnable art resting on definite principles derived from the nature of human consciousness. Because knowledge is conceptual (shorthand for concretes), contextual (relational), and limited (the "crow epistemology"), communicators must master specific techniques to make their ideas clear, persuasive, and objective.

The book covers three forms of communication -- **writing**, **speaking**, and **arguing** -- applying the same foundational principles to each while addressing their distinct requirements.

## Master Action-Item Checklist

### Before You Begin (Planning)

- [ ] **Define your audience.** Who are they? What do they know? What do they care about? What errors might confuse them?
- [ ] **Identify the "so what?"** Why should this audience care about your topic? Prepare a clear motivational statement.
- [ ] **Delimit ruthlessly.** List the bare minimum (what must be included) and the optional (what can be cut). Guiltlessly set aside everything else.
- [ ] **Create an outline.** Even a "matchbox outline" of 3-5 key words. Know the logical order of your points and be able to defend it.
- [ ] **Check self-containment.** For each point: does this raise questions I cannot answer here? If yes, reformulate, make a glancing reference, or excise.
- [ ] **Plan concretisation.** For each key abstraction, prepare at least one concrete example. For each group of examples, prepare an integrating abstraction.

### While Writing

- [ ] **Write freely first.** Get content out without self-censorship. Do not edit while creating.
- [ ] **Then edit as a stranger.** Read your work as if written by someone else. Ask: What has this person actually said? What could be legitimately misinterpreted?
- [ ] **Check formulations.** Is every statement clear, specific, and free of misleading implications?
- [ ] **Check placement.** Does each statement's position in the text imply only what you intend?
- [ ] **Check for rationalism.** Does every deductive chain start from observed reality? Does the conclusion match facts?
- [ ] **Check for floating abstractions.** Can you point to concrete instances for every key term?

### While Speaking

- [ ] **Simplify language.** Short sentences, common words, independent clauses. The harder the subject, the simpler the language.
- [ ] **Repeat key points** in different formulations. Sum up after each section.
- [ ] **Signal every transition.** Tell the audience when you move from one point to the next.
- [ ] **Monitor the audience.** Watch for signs of confusion or boredom. Adapt in real time.
- [ ] **Use emphasis.** Not all statements are equal. Signal which are important through pace, volume, and intensity.
- [ ] **Keep timing marks** in your notes. Cut optional material rather than talking faster.

### While Arguing

- [ ] **Push toward fundamentals.** Keep asking "Why?" until you reach the basic philosophic disagreement.
- [ ] **Never concede a premise you reject**, even "for the sake of argument."
- [ ] **Select one essential from any barrage** and deny the rest sweepingly.
- [ ] **Isolate philosophy from facts.** Ask: "If the facts were different, would you change your position?"
- [ ] **Invoke the onus of proof.** You do not have to prove a negative.
- [ ] **Conduct a private post-mortem** after every argument.

### Always

- [ ] **Separate performance from evaluation.** When performing, forget the rules. When evaluating, apply them.
- [ ] **Learn one principle at a time.** Do not try to hold all principles in mind simultaneously.
- [ ] **Treat communication as an act of self-assertion**, not as dutiful rule-following.
