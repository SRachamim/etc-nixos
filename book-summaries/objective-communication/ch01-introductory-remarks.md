# Chapter 1: Introductory Remarks

**Book:** *Objective Communication: Writing, Speaking, and Arguing* -- Leonard Peikoff (ed. Barry Wood)

## Core Thesis

Communication is the act of making one's thought known to others. It presupposes the ability to think. Today's anti-reason culture has devastated both thought and communication, making the skills of effective communication more urgent than ever. These skills are neither innate nor automatic -- they rest on definite, learnable principles.

## Key Ideas

### The Crisis of Communication

Every element of reason is under relentless cultural attack:

- **Senses** are dismissed as subjective, with mystic alternatives (ESP, revelation) offered instead.
- **Concepts** are treated as arbitrary social conventions with no right or wrong usage.
- **Logic** is presented as a game with arbitrary rules, producing a proliferation of "alternative logics."
- **Objectivity** is declared impossible -- each person lives in a "private world" guided only by feeling.

The result: institutionalised illiteracy. Students cannot spell, define words, organise thoughts into paragraphs, or structure a logical argument.

### Three Categories of People Affected

1. **The wrecked** -- passive, inarticulate, unable or unwilling to contest what is going on.
2. **The escapists** -- better people who flee ideas altogether into engineering, business, or science, resigning from the intellectual battle.
3. **The best** -- those who accept better ideas and try to fight for reason, but are hampered by absorbed subjectivity and a lack of training in thought and communication.

### The Relationship Between Thought and Communication

Communication is not merely the output of thought -- it actively improves thinking. Attempting to present an idea to others forces you to:

- Determine where to start
- Identify what the idea rests on
- Discover questions and implications you had not considered
- Integrate new material into the rest of your knowledge

> "I have often found that I do not really understand something clearly until I try to give a formal presentation of it to others."

### Communication as a Learnable Art

In the ancient world, this art was called **rhetoric** -- the art of presenting ideas persuasively. It was treated as a serious discipline. Today, it is not effectively taught. The principles exist and can be defined, applied, and practised.

## Practical Action Items

1. **Treat communication as a skill to develop deliberately.** Do not assume you know how to present ideas just because you understand them. The ability to communicate clearly is distinct from understanding content.

2. **Use communication as a tool for learning.** When you encounter a new idea, do not passively absorb it. Try to present it to someone else -- this forces you to identify gaps in your understanding.

3. **Expect difficulty and accept it.** Communication is complex and requires effort. The fact that it feels hard does not mean you are failing; it means you are engaging with the actual challenge.

4. **Recognise the stakes.** The spread of correct ideas depends on advocates who both understand them clearly and can communicate them effectively -- in any form: conversation, writing, speaking, argument.

5. **Do not withdraw from the field of ideas.** Retreating into "safe" technical domains may preserve your sanity but does nothing to challenge the entrenched anti-reason viewpoint.
