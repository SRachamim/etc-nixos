# Chapter 2: Philosophic Bases of Communication

**Book:** *Objective Communication: Writing, Speaking, and Arguing* -- Leonard Peikoff (ed. Barry Wood)

## Core Thesis

Effective communication rests on three philosophic principles from epistemology and metaphysics: (1) knowledge is conceptual, (2) knowledge is contextual, and (3) consciousness is limited (the "crow epistemology"). Understanding these is the prerequisite for every communication technique that follows.

## The Three Principles

### 1. Knowledge Is Conceptual

All human thought and communication operates through **concepts** -- mental integrations that condense vast numbers of percepts into single units (words). The word "table" subsumes all tables past, present, and future into one holdable unit.

**The problem this creates:** Unlike perception, which is automatically clear and tied to reality, concepts are a complex shorthand that is:
- Not automatically clear
- Not necessarily formed correctly
- Not necessarily tied to reality

When people learn words without grounding them in actual facts, the result is **floating abstractions** -- terms with no real connection to reality. (Example: most people's understanding of "freedom" collapses under questioning.)

**Implication for communicators:** Clarity must be *achieved* through deliberate techniques. You must keep your audience tied to reality at each point. This involves far more than just defining terms -- it includes decisions about where to start, what to include or omit, what confusions to address, what order to follow, and when and how many examples to give.

### 2. Knowledge Is Contextual

Knowledge is not a collection of disconnected pieces. It is a relational structure where each item connects to others, and every new point is understood in terms of what has come before -- its **context**.

**Example:** The sentence "All human beings have a right to life" means radically different things depending on whether it was said by a follower of Thomas Jefferson, an advocate of socialised medicine, an anti-abortionist, a pacifist, or an opponent of capital punishment. Context determines interpretation.

**Implication for communicators:** You must know your audience's context:
- What do they already know?
- What ideas will govern how they interpret what you say?
- What errors might confuse them?
- What obvious points would bore them if repeated?

Good structure means each point creates the necessary context for the next, so that what follows is clear and understandable.

### 3. The Crow Epistemology (Consciousness Is Limited)

From the metaphysical principle A is A -- everything that exists has a specific, limited identity -- it follows that consciousness, too, is limited. It can deal with only so many units at one time. Beyond that limit, content becomes a blur and the mind cannot encompass it.

**The crow experiment:** Crows can distinguish up to about three units; beyond that, quantities become "lots." Humans are better, but fundamentally the same principle applies. After a certain number of units, we lose track.

**Implication for communicators:** You must always remember that your audience can retain only so many units. It is your job to:
- Economise units, reducing the number wherever possible
- Avoid overloading listeners/readers with too many points before tying them up
- Strike a balance between being clear and being economical

**What happens when you violate this:** The audience's grasp breaks down, they tune out, and communication ends. Once they give up, getting them back is extremely difficult.

This applies on every level -- from a single sentence (Kant's monstrous sentences are an example) to an entire presentation.

## Practical Action Items

1. **Ground every key concept in reality.** Before using an abstract term, ask: can I point to concrete instances? If you cannot, you are trafficking in floating abstractions.

2. **Define your audience's context before you begin.** Ask: What do they know? What will they misunderstand? What can I take for granted? What must I explain? This determines everything about your presentation.

3. **Structure your material so each point prepares for the next.** Each step should create the context required for the audience to understand what follows.

4. **Respect the crow epistemology ruthlessly.** Count the number of unresolved units you are asking your audience to hold. If you are giving them more than they can retain before tying things up, simplify or restructure.

5. **Monitor for the "snap."** Pay attention to the point where your audience (or you yourself, when reading something) hits capacity. That is the crow epistemology asserting itself, and it requires immediate action: summarise, integrate, or reduce.

6. **Balance clarity and economy.** More explanation is not always better. You must set context without overloading, give enough without drowning. This is a balancing act that takes practice.
