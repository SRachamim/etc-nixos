# Chapter 4: Keeping Material Self-Contained

**Book:** *Objective Communication: Writing, Speaking, and Arguing* -- Leonard Peikoff (ed. Barry Wood)

## Core Thesis

Self-containment is a crucial specification of the delimitation principle. The rule: **include only that which you have room to explain or clarify, given your time and audience.** Do not include issues, statements, or examples that directly raise legitimate questions you are unable to deal with. If something would confuse the audience and you cannot explain it within the scope of your presentation, excise it ruthlessly.

## Key Ideas

### The Self-Containment Principle

- Only directly raised questions count -- every piece indirectly raises every question, since all knowledge is interconnected.
- Only legitimate questions from a rational audience count -- no piece can be impregnable against hostile distortions.
- The tension: you want to give a strong case (pushing you to include more), but past a certain point, you spill over into the un-self-contained.
- This is a balancing act learned through experience.

### How to Judge What Needs Explanation

- **Use experience:** Talk to people and notice when they become baffled. Patterns emerge.
- **Use autobiography:** Ask "What was unclear to me when I first learned this? What would I have liked to have been told?" -- but filter for the extra confusion acquired from modern education.
- **Use your audience as the standard:** Some things are common sense and need no elaboration (e.g., "The world does not owe you a living"). Others are contextual -- what requires explanation in one era may be obvious in another.

### Glancing References

When you must comment on something without fully covering it, make a **glancing reference**: explicitly acknowledge the issue and state that you are not covering it here. This puts your cards on the table.

Example: "I am addressing you on the premise that man has free will. If you want to argue about that, do it later."

### Formulations Matter as Much as Topics

Self-containment applies not only to what *topics* you include, but to *how you word* individual statements. A careless formulation can raise questions you cannot answer within your scope.

**Example from the book:** If Rand had added "That is why they are so unhappy" early in the West Point speech, it would have required a whole dissertation on happiness before she had even explained what philosophy is. Instead, she later achieved a glancing reference to happiness ("not very active, not very confident, not very happy") that was prepared by the preceding context.

### The Moral-Practical Integration

When your moral point raises practical questions, address both. If you have no space for a full treatment, at least *indicate* that a practical answer exists.

- Moral-only leaves the implication that you are a mere theoretician.
- Practical-only leaves the question: "Why must it work? Is there any theoretical basis?"
- Combining both is very powerful.

## Extended Example: Rand's "The Wreckage of the Consensus" vs. a Deliberately Bad Draft

The chapter contrasts two articles on the military draft:

### The Good Example (Rand)

- Pitches the argument at the level of **individual rights** -- not at the level of "the basic standard of morality" or "the Law of Identity," which would require covering far more complex issues first.
- Uses contrast with liberal and conservative views to clarify her own position without needing a full theoretical discussion.
- Refutes the "rights impose obligations" argument on the level already established (rights vs. gifts), without needing to discuss the full theory of altruism.
- Addresses practical questions about a volunteer military.
- Builds up to revealing the actual motive of draft advocates only after establishing that the draft is immoral, impractical, and indefensible -- so the conclusion is prepared, not arbitrary.

### The Bad Example (Peikoff's deliberate exercise in non-self-containment)

Every paragraph digs a deeper hole:

- Gratuitous attack on religion -- unnecessary and unexplained.
- "The war is not really that important" -- can be read as "ideas matter more than mass slaughter."
- Pitches the argument at "man's life as the standard of value" -- far more complex and controversial than the draft itself, requiring extensive elaboration that is not given.
- Introduces "altruism," "selfishness," "objectivity," and "logic" without preparation.
- "Money is the real issue" -- implies nothing ideological matters, only finances.
- "The right to life is more important than the right to property" -- a self-inflicted mortal wound that implies we can sacrifice property rights when convenient.
- The conclusion about the evil of draft advocates is completely unprepared and arbitrary.

**Key lesson:** You can say the truth and nothing but the truth, yet your entire presentation can collapse because of its *form* -- because you raised questions you could not answer.

## Practical Action Items

1. **Before including any point, ask: Does this directly raise questions I cannot deal with here?** If yes, either (a) reformulate to avoid raising the question, (b) make a glancing reference acknowledging the issue, or (c) excise it entirely.

2. **Pitch your argument at the right level of abstraction.** Do not go deeper than you have room to explain. If "individual rights" is understandable to your audience and self-contained, use it -- do not go all the way back to "the Law of Identity."

3. **Test every individual formulation, not just your topic list.** A single careless sentence ("The war is not really that important") can sink an otherwise sound presentation.

4. **When making a moral point, address practical implications.** At minimum, indicate that a practical answer exists, even if you cannot elaborate fully.

5. **Build up to controversial conclusions.** Do not state shocking conclusions before you have prepared the ground. A conclusion that is luminously clear in one context is arbitrary and alienating in another.

6. **Use contrast to clarify without lengthy explanation.** Setting your view against competing views can make your position clearer without requiring a full theoretical discussion.

7. **After writing, review as an outsider.** Read your own work as if you had no inside knowledge. Ask: "What legitimate questions does each sentence raise? Are they answered?"
