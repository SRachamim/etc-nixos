# Chapter 5: Rationalism

**Book:** *Objective Communication: Writing, Speaking, and Arguing* -- Leonard Peikoff (ed. Barry Wood)

## Core Thesis

Rationalism -- deduction without reference to reality -- is the most widespread and harmful error of today's intellectuals. It consists of seizing on some plausible-sounding premise in a vacuum and then deducing consequences from it, producing an elaborate logical structure that floats entirely disconnected from observed facts.

## Key Ideas

### What Rationalism Is

The essence: **ignoring reality, ignoring observational evidence, seizing on some idea in a vacuum, and deducing consequences from it.**

- The result looks imposing -- long chains of "if this, then this; therefore QED" -- but it all rests on nothing.
- It often culminates in conclusions blatantly contrary to observed facts, but the rationalist says, "I have proved my conclusion; so much the worse for the facts."
- The rationalist's primary concern is the relation of one idea to another idea, not the relation of ideas to reality.
- Concepts become a world of their own -- a Platonic world of Forms, cut off from the concretes they are supposed to represent.

### Rationalism vs. Proper Reasoning

Objectivism holds that knowledge is the integration of **logic and experience** -- not an either/or. Rationalism severs logic from experience and treats deduction alone as sufficient.

Ayn Rand's illustrative example: A rationalist argues "Man has only two eyes; therefore he should see only two things." Two schools arise: one claims men really do see only two things (everything else is illusion); the other claims men have hidden eyes. Neither bothers to look at reality.

### Two Forms of Rationalism

1. **Positive rationalism:** Using the method to "prove" your own position. Start with an arbitrary premise, deduce a chain of consequences, arrive at a conclusion that defies reality.

2. **Negative rationalism (rationalist polemics):** Using the method to refute an opponent. Accept the opponent's basic premise, then show he contradicts himself internally. This is inadvisable because:
   - You train yourself to focus on your opponent's ideas rather than on reality.
   - You widen the breach between your mind and reality.
   - Once you accept the opponent's premises, he sets the terms, and you are at his mercy.

### Rationalism as Rationalisation

Theologians exemplify this: they decide the conclusion in advance (by revelation), then cook up arguments to reach it, juggling ideas until they seem to justify the preordained conclusion. This is not reasoning; it is rationalisation.

### How to Detect Rationalism

Look for this combination:
- **Very abstract content** (disconnected from concrete observation)
- **A deductive chain** ("if this, then this; therefore...")
- **Collapse when you ask "Why?" at a crucial point** -- the entire structure dissolves

In real life, rationalists are rarely pure. They smuggle in facts as needed, but since the facts are not properly ordered or contextualised, they are unconvincing and dissolve at the first challenge.

## Extended Example: A Paper on Certainty

Peikoff analyses a paper he himself wrote as a graduate student (Appendix B), which argues for certainty against scepticism purely through rationalist polemics.

### Two ways the paper is fundamentally disconnected from reality:

1. **No consideration of why anyone would be a sceptic.** No arguments for scepticism are examined. Reality is irrelevant -- the author just starts with "my opponent has a viewpoint" and tries to deduce contradictions.

2. **No positive argument for certainty.** No definition or characterisation of certainty is given. The author is not concerned with what certainty *is* or what facts establish it -- only with showing that denying it leads to contradictions.

### Why the arguments fail:

- The infinite regress argument ("Are you certain you're uncertain?") can be turned equally against the advocate of certainty ("Are you certain of your certainty?"). Only by establishing that certainty is inherent in consciousness itself can you break the symmetry -- but this positive point is never made.
- The author smuggles in certainties (like the Law of Excluded Middle) without justifying them within his own framework.
- Each "refutation" is met by an obvious sceptical counter that the paper has no resources to answer.

### The lesson:

Rationalist polemics are self-defeating. By conceding the opponent's starting point and trying to trap him internally, you lose the ability to appeal to the facts that would actually settle the question. The proper approach: start from reality, state positively what certainty is, and show why the sceptic's arguments fail when measured against actual facts of consciousness.

## Practical Action Items

1. **Always connect your reasoning to observable reality.** Before constructing a chain of deduction, ask: "What facts am I starting from? Can I point to them?" If your starting premise is not grounded in observation, your entire chain floats.

2. **Watch for the combination of high abstraction + deductive chain.** This is the signature of rationalism. When you see it in your own work or others', ask "Why?" at the starting premise. If it collapses, it is rationalist.

3. **Do not rely on rationalist polemics.** Do not make a habit of accepting your opponent's premises to show he contradicts himself. Instead, challenge the premises directly by reference to reality.

4. **Give positive arguments, not just negative refutations.** State what you believe and why, grounded in observable facts. Merely showing that denying your view leads to contradictions is insufficient.

5. **Beware of smuggled premises.** If your argument suddenly introduces an undefended certainty (a law of logic, a definition, a factual claim), recognise that you have implicitly assumed something you have not argued for.

6. **Ask "Does this deduction correspond to actual facts?"** After constructing a logical chain, check: does the conclusion match what you can observe? If it contradicts reality, the error is in your premises or your reasoning, not in reality.

7. **Inoculate yourself.** Even if you do not commit rationalism yourself, understanding it lets you diagnose why so much intellectual output -- from professors, publications, and polemics -- sounds impressive but proves nothing.
