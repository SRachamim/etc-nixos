# Chapter 6: Objectivity

**Book:** *Objective Communication: Writing, Speaking, and Arguing* -- Leonard Peikoff (ed. Barry Wood)

## Core Thesis

Objective communication means communication in which you have adhered to reality so thoroughly -- in organising, delimiting, formulating, contextualising, and concretising -- that the result **stands on its own**, independent of you. Its meaning and validity are clear without any further intervention by you. A subjective presentation, by contrast, needs the writer to rush in and explain what was actually meant.

## Key Ideas

### Objectivity in Metaphysics and Epistemology

- **Metaphysical objectivity:** Facts are what they are, independent of consciousness. A is A.
- **Epistemological objectivity:** Adhering to reality in one's mental processes. Being guided by facts, not by feelings, desires, or arbitrary preferences.

### Objectivity in Communication

An objective presentation meets this standard: you do not have to rush in each time someone reads a sentence and say "No, I meant so-and-so" or "Oh, I forgot to include this." The presentation is like an external fact -- an object apart from you.

A subjective presentation needs continual clarification, elaboration, or defence of what you actually meant.

### Three Categories of Non-Objective Formulations

#### 1. Arbitrary Statements (Not Self-Contained)

Saying something unprepared, unexplained, and undefended. The audience does not see why you are saying it, so the statement does not stand on its own.

If a point is significant, you must defend it in some terms. You cannot prove everything, but important claims need at least some reason.

#### 2. Poor Formulation (Unclear Wording)

Statements that can legitimately be taken in different ways:
- **Vague and undefined:** "Having the right idea about men" -- what does "right" mean?
- **Ambiguous:** Multiple possible meanings at the same time
- **Misleading implications:** Commits you to something you would never accept (e.g., "The war is not that important" implying ideas matter more than mass slaughter)

**Example:** "In a proper capitalist system, no individual can exert a deleterious influence on the achievement of any other person's rightful values." The word "deleterious" is so vague it could include legitimate competition, implying a need for anti-competition laws. The actual point (no initiation of force) is lost.

Even punctuation matters: "In a proper capitalist system" (no comma) implies there is also an "improper capitalist system." "In a proper, capitalist system" (with comma) means a system that is both proper and capitalist.

#### 3. Poor Placement (Misleading Context)

The formulation itself may be true and clear, but its *position* in the text makes it imply something the author does not mean.

**Example:** In a refutation of Hume's pleasure-based ethics, the author says "Thinking is painful for many people, yet thought is man's primary tool of survival." The placement implies: "The ethics I advocate is oblivious to pleasure. Pain does not matter; only life's requirements count." This commits the author to the view that man is intrinsically at war with reality -- that life and pleasure point in opposite directions. Moving this statement to a different position (after firmly establishing what is universal about human nature) would eliminate the problem entirely.

### The Method of Achieving Objectivity

1. **Write first, then check.** Get what is in your mind out on paper. Then throw a switch.
2. **Read as an outsider.** Set aside everything you know internally. Look only at what is actually on paper.
3. **Ask: "What could a rational person legitimately misinterpret?"** Not hostile distortion, but genuine reasonable misreading.
4. **Treat your writing like a legal document.** Read with the seriousness you would apply to a contract you are about to sign. Mean what you say; aim for precision in statement and implication.

### Rationalism as a Form of Non-Objectivity

The chapter includes several examples of rationalistic arguments that appear rigorous but float free of reality:

- A passage on women's literature that "proves" attacks on women are really attacks on men -- through a deductive chain that ignores whether the deduction corresponds to actual human psychology.
- A passage on free will that tries to prove man *must* have free will through speculative deduction, rather than pointing to the directly perceivable fact that one can choose to focus or not.
- A passage on morality that assumes a society without moral concepts could exist, thereby conceding at the outset the very question of whether morality is necessary.

### Package Deals

One example shows how an undefined key term creates a **package deal** -- combining two distinct concepts under one label. A paper on "spoon-feeding" in education conflates "making material too easy" with "the lecturer giving his own evaluations." Without defining the term, the author can oscillate between attacking one and the other, never confronting the fact that giving evaluations can stimulate more thinking, not less.

**Moral:** If a term is central to your presentation, you must have a firm definition in your own mind. Otherwise, you can say contradictory things while appearing consistent.

## Practical Action Items

1. **After writing, read your work as a stranger would.** Cut yourself off from everything in your mind that you did not put on paper. Ask: "What has this person actually said?"

2. **Check every formulation for three things:** What does it say? Why does it say it? Where does it say it? (Formulation, defence, and context.)

3. **Watch for misleading implications.** Ask of each statement: "What does this commit me to? What would a reasonable person conclude from this?" Fix any unintended implications immediately.

4. **Do not use undefined key terms.** If a concept is central to your argument, define it explicitly. An undefined term lets you (and others) equivocate without realising it.

5. **Test placement by imagining the statement moved.** Would the same sentence mean something different if placed earlier or later? If so, check that its current position conveys only what you intend.

6. **Do not expect perfection in rough drafts.** Subjective formulations are inevitable in first drafts. The skill is in catching and fixing them during editing. Keep the performance (writing) and the evaluation (editing) separate.

7. **Beware of polemics that concede too much.** If you are refuting an opponent by accepting their premise ("Even on your terms..."), check whether the concession commits you to something you reject. A polemic that wins the argument at the price of implying a theory you do not subscribe to is not objective.
