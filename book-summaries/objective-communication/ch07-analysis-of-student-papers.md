# Chapter 7: Analysis of Student Papers

**Book:** *Objective Communication: Writing, Speaking, and Arguing* -- Leonard Peikoff (ed. Barry Wood)

## Core Thesis

This chapter applies all the principles from chapters 1-6 to actual student papers, providing a master class in diagnosis. The papers are critiqued for violations of motivation, delimitation, self-containment, structure, concretisation, objectivity, and rationalism. The analysis demonstrates that these are not abstract rules but tools that produce concrete, identifiable improvements.

## Key Patterns and Lessons

### Common Errors in Student Writing

1. **Starting too abstractly.** Many papers plunge into philosophical content without any motivating context. The reader has no reason to care and no anchor in reality.

2. **Non-self-contained formulations.** Statements that are true in the writer's mind but raise unanswerable questions for the uninitiated reader. The writer knows the full context; the reader does not.

3. **Rationalistic argument.** Deductive chains that float free of reality. The structure appears logical, but the starting premises are ungrounded and the conclusions contradict observable facts.

4. **Overstatement.** Claims that go beyond what the evidence or context supports (e.g., calling someone "the most immoral person in history" without justification).

5. **Undefined key terms.** Central concepts used without definition, permitting unintended interpretations and package deals.

6. **Poor placement.** True statements in contexts that make them imply something the author would reject.

7. **Missing motivation.** Papers that assume the reader already cares about the topic, without establishing why it matters.

### What Good Student Writing Looks Like

The chapter also highlights papers with strengths:

- **Forthright openings** that lay down the author's terms unapologetically
- **Clear structure** with numbered points
- **Self-contained formulations** that make their meaning accessible without external clarification
- **Appropriate concretisation** that grounds abstract points in recognisable examples

### The Editing Process

The chapter repeatedly demonstrates that most errors are **rough draft errors** -- natural products of the writing process that should be caught in editing. The key steps:

1. Write freely -- let ideas pour out without self-censorship.
2. Then switch to editor mode -- read as a stranger.
3. Ask of each sentence: Is this clear? Self-contained? Could it be legitimately misinterpreted? Does it raise questions I have not answered?
4. Fix the problems and repeat.

## Practical Action Items

1. **Write your rough draft without editing.** Attempting to perfect while creating is paralysing. Get the content out first.

2. **Edit with specific checkpoints.** For each paragraph, ask:
   - Is the motivation clear? (Why should the reader care about this point?)
   - Is it self-contained? (Can the reader understand it without knowing what is in my head?)
   - Are key terms defined?
   - Could any statement be legitimately misread?
   - Is the placement of each claim in the right context?

3. **Watch for overstatement.** If you write something like "X is the worst/most/greatest," ask whether you can defend that absolute claim. If not, moderate it.

4. **Test your opening.** Read only your first paragraph. Would a sceptical but rational reader continue? If not, strengthen the motivation before proceeding.

5. **Look for rationalism in your arguments.** After constructing any chain of reasoning, ask: "Is my starting point grounded in observation? Does my conclusion match reality? Would asking 'Why?' at any step collapse the chain?"

6. **Have someone else read your work.** The single most reliable way to discover non-self-contained formulations is to see another person's genuine confusion about what you meant.
