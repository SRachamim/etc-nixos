# Chapter 8: Principles of Speaking

**Book:** *Objective Communication: Writing, Speaking, and Arguing* -- Leonard Peikoff (ed. Barry Wood)

## Core Thesis

Oral extemporaneous delivery is a distinct form of communication that follows all the same principles as writing (motivation, delimitation, structure, concretisation, objectivity) but differs crucially in **pace**, **exactness of formulation**, and the ability to **monitor the audience** in real time. The speaker must slow down, simplify, repeat, and adapt -- all while the audience can take in less than readers can.

## Key Differences Between Speaking and Writing

### 1. Pace (Slower Is Essential)

Listeners can retain fewer units than readers because:
- **Readers control their own pace** -- they can pause, reread, rest, and reflect at will.
- **Listeners are at the speaker's mercy** -- one moment of mental pause means missing the next sentence.
- **Hearing is harder than seeing** -- sight grasps entities (solid, permanent), hearing grasps fleeting sounds (transitory attributes). Hearing ideas requires more intense concentration.

Therefore: the actual rate of data must be less in speaking than in writing.

**How to reduce pace:**

1. **Simplify grammar and vocabulary.** Use short, independent clauses instead of long subordinate structures. Use colloquial words ("disaster" before "cataclysm"). Be more colloquial, not less -- the harder the subject, the simpler the language.

2. **Anticipate the audience's need to pause.** When you know a formulation will puzzle or impress, stop for a moment. It rarely feels as long to the audience as it does to you.

3. **Repeat liberally.** In writing, repetition is generally confusing. In speaking, it is imperative. Circle around your point -- say it several ways until one gets through. Sum up frequently. Review backward references ("As we said..." followed by a quick reminder).

4. **Make transitions unmistakable.** In writing, smooth invisible transitions are a virtue. In speaking, the reverse: reveal your skeleton. State explicitly: "We have finished X; now we turn to Y." Break material into ~10-minute units and signal each transition. Numbering is valuable. Each transition is a chance to recover listeners you have lost.

5. **Use emphasis.** Not all statements are equal -- signal which are important. Slow down, get louder, increase intensity for key points. Throw away lesser points more quickly and quietly. "Do not be egalitarian in your delivery."

### 2. Exactness of Formulation (Circling Around)

In writing, you have outline → rough draft → editing. In speaking, your notes are the outline, and the talk itself is the rough draft. There is no editing stage.

The result: a necessary process of **self-monitoring and self-correction**. You say something, realise it could be misinterpreted, and immediately restate: "Or, to put it another way..." You circle around the point from different angles until you feel it is clear.

This roughness is **not fatal to objectivity** -- the audience understands that extemporaneous delivery involves groping. In writing, "Money is the real issue" would be a grave error. In speaking, the audience forgives imprecise formulations as long as the main point emerges.

There is a natural harmony: the speaker's need to circle matches the audience's need for a slower pace.

### 3. Monitoring the Audience (Real-Time Feedback)

Unlike writing, in extemporaneous delivery you can see your audience's reactions and adapt on the spot. This is a crucial advantage.

**How to monitor:**
- Maintain eye contact -- rove your gaze across the room, do not fixate on one responsive face.
- Watch for indicators: yawns, coughs, shuffling, glazed eyes, restless energy, quality of silence during pauses.
- Monitor two things: (a) do they understand? (b) do they care?
- Audience reaction is not infallible, but it is useful data.

**How to adapt when losing the audience:**

1. **Change your pace.** If they look blank: add an example, insert a summary, repeat more simply. If they are ahead of you: cut your prepared elaboration and move on.

2. **Reestablish motivation.** If the underlying problem is indifference, not confusion: stop completely. Spend time explaining why the topic matters. It is better to sacrifice half your content than to continue to a bored audience.

3. **Use humour.** A laugh temporarily opens the mental door. A loud laugh buys 3-5 minutes of attention; a chuckle ~1 minute; a smile ~20 seconds. Lighten dry or technical material with funny examples, jokes, or ad-lib wisecracks. Project: "It is okay to enjoy this."

4. **Get the audience to participate.** Ask questions. Let people speak. The act of participating amplifies interest. But keep it balanced -- do not let it become an unstructured bull session. Spread participation around; do not let one person monopolise.

## The Role of Notes

Strike a balance:
- **Too brief:** You will get stranded mid-talk with no idea what comes next.
- **Too detailed:** You will revert to reading, losing the benefits of extemporaneous delivery.

**What to write down:**
- The main divisions of your outline (enough to keep yourself on track)
- Key formulations that must be exact
- For the first minute or two (especially when inexperienced), write out sentences to counteract nervousness

**Practical technique:** Keep your finger moving down your notes as you proceed, so you can glance down and immediately find your place without searching.

## Timing

- **Mark estimated times in your notes** (in a different colour, like red) next to where you should be at each point.
- Keep a watch visible.
- **If running long:** Do not talk faster (the audience cannot absorb faster). Start cutting optional points from your notes. As a last resort, confess and stop.
- **If running short:** Expand with examples, elaboration, or reserve material. Always keep ~20 minutes of reserve material you have no intention of using.
- **Practice at home:** Deliver points to a blank wall, timed. Try the same point in 5 minutes, then 2 minutes, then 30 seconds.

## Reading Aloud from a Prepared Text

Advantages: exact formulations, no groping. Disadvantages: pace must still suit a listening audience (hard to achieve in fully written form), and you lose adaptability.

A skilled speaker can **fake extemporaneity** when reading -- deliberately hesitating as though searching for words, reenacting the process of thinking. This makes a prepared text feel live.

## Additional Factors

- **Enthusiasm and confidence in voice:** Communicate energy and commitment. Even act enthusiastic if you do not feel it spontaneously.
- **Volume:** If they have to strain to hear, you are finished. Belt it out. Especially important for women speakers.
- **Mannerisms:** The more motionless you stand, the better. Weaving, fidgeting, and tics distract from content.
- **Environment:** Temperature (cold is better), lighting, acoustics, smoke, microphone quality all matter.
- **Fatigue:** Give intermissions. Do not go on too long.

## Practical Action Items

1. **Strip your talk down to essentials.** Speaking must be more single-track than writing. If your notes would make a good article, they are too detailed for a talk.

2. **Simplify your language ruthlessly.** Short sentences, common words, independent clauses. The harder the subject, the simpler the language.

3. **Build in repetition deliberately.** Plan to say key points 2-3 times in different ways. Sum up after each major section.

4. **Signal every transition.** Tell the audience when you move from one point to the next. Number your points. Each transition is a re-engagement opportunity.

5. **Prepare your notes with timing marks.** Estimate how long each section will take. Mark checkpoints on your notes in red. Keep a watch visible.

6. **Practise to a wall.** Deliver your talk out loud, timed, before giving it live. You will be amazed at the discrepancy between your estimate and reality.

7. **Keep reserve material.** Have ~20 minutes of optional content you probably will not use, in case you run short.

8. **Look at your audience.** Rove your gaze. Monitor comprehension and interest. Adapt in real time.

9. **When you feel you are losing them, stop and diagnose.** Is it pace? Motivation? Fatigue? Match the remedy to the cause.

10. **Do not talk faster when running long.** Instead, cut optional material. The audience cannot absorb faster than their natural pace.
