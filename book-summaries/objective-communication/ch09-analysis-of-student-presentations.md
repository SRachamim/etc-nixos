# Chapter 9: Analysis of Student Presentations

**Book:** *Objective Communication: Writing, Speaking, and Arguing* -- Leonard Peikoff (ed. Barry Wood)

## Core Thesis

This chapter applies the principles of oral communication (from Chapter 8) to actual student presentations, demonstrating how violations of pace, motivation, self-containment, and structure manifest in live delivery and how to fix them. The analysis serves as a practical workshop on the difference between writing ideas and speaking them.

## Key Patterns and Lessons

### Common Speaking Errors Observed

1. **Pace too fast / too much material.** Students try to cram written-level density into oral delivery. The audience cannot absorb it. Solution: strip down to essentials, repeat key points, add summaries.

2. **Insufficient motivation.** Speakers launch into technical content without establishing why the audience should care. Even a brief "here is why this matters" opening makes a dramatic difference.

3. **Reading rather than speaking.** Students who write out their talks word-for-word tend to read in a monotone, lose eye contact, and fail to monitor audience reaction. Extemporaneous delivery, even if rougher, is more engaging.

4. **No transitions or signposting.** Listeners get lost when the speaker moves from one topic to another without signalling. Even a simple "Now let us turn to..." restores orientation.

5. **Abstract without concrete.** Just as in writing, speakers who stay at a high level of abstraction without examples lose their audience. But in speaking, this problem is worse because the listener cannot reread.

6. **No self-monitoring.** Speakers who do not check their own formulations in real time miss opportunities to clarify or correct misleading statements.

### What Good Student Speaking Looks Like

- **Clear, simple sentence structure** adapted to the listening audience
- **Frequent repetition and summarisation** without it feeling tedious
- **Eye contact and audience monitoring** -- adjusting pace, adding examples, or pausing based on audience response
- **Enthusiasm and energy** that communicates "this matters"
- **Explicit structure** -- numbering points, signalling transitions

### Key Insight: The Same Content Requires Different Form

Material that works beautifully in a written essay can fail completely in oral delivery. The content may be identical, but the form -- sentence length, vocabulary, pace, repetition, transitions -- must be adapted to the different demands of listening vs. reading.

## Practical Action Items

1. **Never read a written essay aloud as your "talk."** Convert it to speaking form: shorter sentences, simpler words, built-in repetition, explicit transitions.

2. **Practise the conversion.** Take a paragraph of your written work and rephrase it as you would speak it to a friend. Notice how much simpler and more repetitive it becomes.

3. **Open with motivation, every time.** Even if you think the audience knows why they are there, explicitly state why your topic matters. It costs very little time and pays enormous dividends.

4. **When you see confusion, act immediately.** Do not push through. Stop, simplify, add an example, or ask the audience where they are lost.

5. **Record yourself and review.** The gap between what you think you sound like and what you actually sound like is usually large. Listen for pace, clarity, emphasis, and energy.

6. **Build transitions into your notes.** Between each major point, write a transition phrase. This ensures you signal movement even when nervous.
