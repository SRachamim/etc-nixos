# Chapter 10: Principles of Arguing

**Book:** *Objective Communication: Writing, Speaking, and Arguing* -- Leonard Peikoff (ed. Barry Wood)

## Core Thesis

Informal arguing (unstructured back-and-forth discussion) is basically unavoidable and, though far less effective than formal writing or speaking, can serve two purposes: (1) suggesting a new viewpoint to your opponent or onlookers, and (2) sharpening your own inner clarity. The key strategies are: detect your opponent's basic premises, refuse to concede them, select the essential point to answer from any barrage, and handle spurious "facts" by isolating the philosophic issue.

## When (and When Not) to Argue

### Argue when:
- Your opponent, however confused, is **honest** -- open to rational argument.
- You believe you will gain inner clarity or suggest a new viewpoint.

### Do not argue when:
- Your opponent is **dishonest** -- facts, arguments, and reasoning make no difference to him.
- The person has explicitly rejected reason (says he goes by faith/LSD/feeling regardless of argument).
- The person's manner is consistently evasive, abusive, or chaotic beyond the normal heat of debate.

### How to judge honesty:

**By content:** Certain positions are explicitly anti-reason or anti-value:
- Openly rejecting reason in favour of faith (not mere belief in God, but explicit denial of reason)
- Openly advocating destruction of the able for being able (not mere welfare-statism, but naked egalitarianism)
- Context matters -- advocacy of communism in 1900 vs. today, for example.

**By manner:** Does the person try to answer your points, or evade? Is he calm or screaming? Does he chronically change the subject, drop personal attacks, spew insults? Some heat is normal -- when it becomes extreme, consistent, and blatant, decline to continue.

**Important:** Do not judge too easily that someone is dishonest. Honest errors are common. If you are actually afraid of being demolished in the argument, do not rationalise this as "he is too dishonest to argue with."

## Four Strategies of Argumentation

### 1. Discover Your Opponent's Basic Premises ("Philosophical Detection")

Controversial concretes (the draft, abortion, nuclear power) are the tip of the iceberg. The real issues are the foundational philosophic premises -- metaphysical, epistemological, ethical -- that underlie the concrete position.

**The method: keep asking "Why?"** Push your opponent deeper and deeper until you reach the foundation.

**Example pattern (abortion):**
- Opponent says: "It is murder; the fetus has a right to life."
- You distinguish potential from actual human beings, raise the mother's rights.
- Possible responses reveal different foundations:
  - "I never thought of that" → honest inquiry, pursue
  - "It is all a matter of semantics" → possible epistemological scepticism, probe further
  - "Our duties come from God" → religious metaphysics, pursue *that* or drop it
  - Inconsistent mix of religion and subjectivism → the person is grabbing at random, help organise his alternatives

**The goal:** Either reach a point where you can argue the real issue (the basic premise), or discover that the chasm is too deep to bridge in this setting.

### 2. Do Not Concede Your Opponent's Premises

Never grant your opponent's foundational claims for the sake of polemical advantage. This is rationalist polemics -- you train yourself to fight on his turf, and once you accept his premises, he sets the terms.

**Example (conceding in the abortion debate):**
- Conceding: "I grant that the fetus is a full human being with rights, but for its own happiness you should abort."
- Opponent's devastating reply: "So you condone murder whenever the victim would be unhappy."
- You have lost irrecoverably because you accepted his basic premise.

**Example (capitalism):**
- Conceding: "We agree that people should serve the public welfare, but capitalism provides the best incentive."
- Opponent's reply: "Then if capitalists do not serve the public, we will force them."
- You have surrendered the moral ground.

**The pattern:** Intellectual appeasement leads to the same result as political or military appeasement. Challenge the basic premise directly and boldly.

### 3. Select the Essential Point to Answer

Your opponent will hurl multiple objections simultaneously. You cannot answer them all -- the crow epistemology applies to argument as well. If you try to address every point, the discussion sprawls and you lose track of everything.

**The method:**
- From the barrage, select the single most essential point.
- Sweepingly deny all the rest: "I disagree with everything you just said, but I will confine myself to point X."
- If you choose X correctly, answering it often lays the base for quick answers to all the lesser points.

**Example (the draft):**
- Three objections: too expensive, unfair to the poor, unpatriotic.
- Choose "unpatriotic" -- it is the purely philosophical one. By establishing that America was founded on individual rights and liberty, you lay the base for quick answers to the other two (cut useless government spending; free the economy so there is ample employment).

**Maintain a mental monitor:** As your opponent speaks, sort each point into "hold," "let go," or "answer this no matter what."

### 4. Counter Spurious "Facts"

Opponents often throw alleged hard evidence -- dubious studies, historical claims, statistics -- that supposedly refute your argument. You cannot become omniscient, and in the vast majority of cases, these "facts" are a smoke screen covering unstated philosophical premises.

**Primary technique: isolate the philosophic issue.**

- Ask: "Is this purely a factual dispute, or is there a deeper disagreement?"
- Test: "If I could prove this fact is wrong, would you then agree with my position?" If not, the "fact" is a tactic, not a viewpoint.
- Switch the discussion from facts to philosophy.

**When you must deal with facts:**

- **Refute on philosophical grounds** when possible (e.g., "extrasensory perception" contradicts cause and effect; "coercive monopoly under capitalism" contradicts laissez-faire by definition).
- **Challenge epistemological credentials.** Ask where the "fact" came from. Modern science's methodology is deeply flawed in many areas. You are entitled to say: "I do not accept your authority, your study, your data."
- **Invoke the onus of proof principle.** The burden of proof is on whoever asserts the positive. An arbitrary claim backed by a dubious authority is no evidence. You are entitled to deny it flatly.

### When You Are Stumped

- **Admit it to yourself.** The most important thing is intellectual honesty with yourself about what you do not know.
- **To your opponent:** Say "I have not heard that before; I will have to think about it." Or: "I know your conclusion is wrong, and here is why, even though I cannot answer that specific argument right now."
- **Ask for slow motion:** "Take it again step by step. Where do you start?" Sometimes walking through the argument slowly reveals the error.
- **Go home and figure it out.** Then apply the answer next time.

## The Mock Debate (Free Will vs. Determinism)

The chapter includes a full mock debate illustrating all principles:

- **Detection:** The determinist equates free will with religion → select the religious equation as the essential to refute first, because it underlies his other objections (science, "vacuum").
- **Not conceding:** When the determinist says "free will is a causeless event," firmly restate that causality and free will are compatible under the right view of cause and effect.
- **Handling facts:** When the determinist cites studies on twins and environment, identify the essential misunderstanding: he conflates *influence* with *determination*.
- **Reaching the foundation:** The determinist eventually reveals he believes science gives only surface regularities, not real truth -- pure Kantianism. Detection is complete. Now you can either pursue the real issue or end the discussion.

## Improving Through Practice

The only way to get better at arguing:

1. **Argue** (where circumstances are appropriate).
2. **Review your performance afterward.** Replay the encounter mentally as an outside observer. Ask:
   - What did I answer right? What wrong?
   - What would I say differently next time?
   - Was there a question I was *afraid* he would ask?
3. **Stock your subconscious with right answers.** You will find they occur to you next time.
4. **Watch others argue.** In the moment between question and answer, prepare your own answer. Compare it to theirs. Learn from the difference.

## Practical Action Items

1. **Always push toward fundamentals.** When arguing a concrete issue, keep asking "Why?" until you reach the basic metaphysical or epistemological disagreement. That is where the real issue lies.

2. **Never concede a premise you reject, even "for the sake of argument."** Challenge it head-on. Intellectual appeasement always loses.

3. **When bombarded with multiple objections, pick one essential and deny the rest.** State clearly: "I disagree with all of that, but let me address this one point."

4. **When confronted with dubious "facts," isolate the philosophy.** Ask: "If the facts were different, would you change your position?" If not, refuse to argue facts and switch to the underlying ideas.

5. **Use the onus of proof principle.** You do not have to prove a negative. If someone asserts something without credible evidence, you are entitled to deny it.

6. **After every argument, conduct a private post-mortem.** What went well? What would you change? What stumped you? Resolve the stumper before the next encounter.

7. **Give the benefit of the doubt.** Do not declare people dishonest too quickly. But once someone explicitly rejects reason, decline to argue. Your refusal is more devastating than any refutation.
