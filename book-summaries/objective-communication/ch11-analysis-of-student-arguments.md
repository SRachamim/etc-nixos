# Chapter 11: Analysis of Student Arguments

**Book:** *Objective Communication: Writing, Speaking, and Arguing* -- Leonard Peikoff (ed. Barry Wood)

## Core Thesis

This chapter applies the principles of argumentation (from Chapter 10) to actual student arguments on topics like compulsory national service, environmentalism, animal rights, abortion, the nature of rights, and more. The analysis demonstrates how to detect premises, avoid concessions, select essentials, and handle dubious facts in live argumentative settings.

## Key Patterns and Lessons

### Common Argumentation Errors Observed

1. **Conceding the opponent's premise.** Students repeatedly accept the moral or epistemological foundation of their opponent's position and then try to win on the opponent's terms. This consistently fails.

2. **Failing to detect the fundamental issue.** Arguments remain stuck at the surface level (specific policy, specific case) rather than drilling down to the basic philosophic disagreement that drives the concrete dispute.

3. **Trying to answer every objection.** Students attempt to address all points simultaneously instead of selecting the one essential and sweepingly denying the rest. The discussion sprawls, the crow epistemology takes over, and nothing is resolved.

4. **Getting mired in factual disputes.** When opponents throw statistics and studies, students engage at that level rather than isolating the underlying philosophy. The result is endless empirical haggling with no resolution.

5. **Overstatement and emotionalism.** Students make claims too extreme to defend, or let emotional intensity substitute for logical precision. Passionate advocacy is fine; undefended assertions are not.

6. **Not admitting when stumped.** Students try to bluff through moments of genuine ignorance rather than honestly saying "I need to think about that." This undermines their credibility and prevents them from learning.

### What Good Student Arguing Looks Like

- **Socratic questioning:** Asking the opponent "Why?" repeatedly and gently until the fundamental premise surfaces.
- **Selective focus:** Choosing one essential point from a barrage and dealing with it thoroughly before moving on.
- **Boldly challenging premises:** Saying "I reject your starting point entirely, and here is why" rather than accepting it for the sake of argument.
- **Maintaining calm control:** Keeping the discussion structured even as the opponent tries to derail it.
- **Honest admission of limits:** "I do not know the answer to that specific point, but I know the conclusion you are drawing from it is wrong."

### Lessons from Specific Arguments

**On compulsory national service:**
- The essential issue is whether the state owns the individual. All practical objections (cost, efficiency, fairness) are secondary.
- Students who concede "the state has *some* claim on you" cannot then defend against any degree of compulsion.

**On environmentalism:**
- The factual claims (pollution levels, species extinction rates, climate projections) are usually a screen for an anti-industrial, anti-human philosophy.
- The productive move: ask "If all environmental problems were solved, would you support unrestricted industrial development?" The answer reveals the real premise.

**On abortion:**
- The argument must be settled at the level of what constitutes a rights-bearing entity (actual vs. potential human being), not at the level of consequences.
- Conceding that the fetus is a person and then arguing for abortion on "quality of life" grounds is fatal.

**On the nature of rights:**
- Students who argue for rights on utilitarian grounds ("rights produce the best outcomes for society") have conceded that rights are contingent on outcomes. Any utilitarian can then show that violating rights sometimes produces "better" outcomes, and the argument collapses.
- Rights must be grounded in the nature of man, not in social consequences.

## Practical Action Items

1. **Before arguing, identify your own non-negotiable premises.** Know in advance what you will not concede under any circumstances. This prevents accidental capitulation in the heat of debate.

2. **Practise the "Why?" technique.** In any argument, push past the surface position to the underlying reason. Then push past that to the reason behind the reason. Three or four levels of "Why?" usually reach the philosophical foundation.

3. **When your opponent gives you multiple objections, explicitly refuse to address all of them.** Say: "You have made several points. I am going to address the most important one. Here it is."

4. **When confronted with facts or studies, ask the revealing question:** "If this fact were different, would you change your position?" If the answer is no, you have proven the fact is a rationalisation, not the actual basis for the view.

5. **Practise arguing positions you hold on topics that matter to you.** After each session, do the private post-mortem: What did I miss? Where was I unclear? What would I say differently?

6. **Study your opponent's most common arguments in advance.** For any topic you expect to argue (e.g., capitalism, individual rights, free will), know the three or four most common objections and have your essential response prepared.
