# Chapter 12: Concluding Remarks

**Book:** *Objective Communication: Writing, Speaking, and Arguing* -- Leonard Peikoff (ed. Barry Wood)

## Core Thesis

Learning to communicate effectively is like learning any skill -- it is difficult at first, there is a lot to absorb, and the danger is paralysis from trying to apply everything at once. The solution: learn gradually, apply the principles one at a time, and -- crucially -- keep the **performance** and the **evaluation** separate.

## Key Ideas

### Do Not Let the Course Paralyse You

- When you first learn the principles of communication, there is a natural feeling of "there is so much here." The mass of considerations can be overwhelming.
- If you try to hold all twelve principles in mind while writing every sentence, you will freeze.
- **The solution:** Digest a little at a time. Apply what you can. Then take a bit more.

### Separate Performance from Evaluation

This is the most important practical advice in the book:

- **When performing** (writing, speaking, or arguing): Forget the principles entirely. Focus only on content. Take the attitude: "I am the master of this subject. There is no one who has ever equalled me."
- **When evaluating** (after the performance is over): Review objectively. Ask: "Is there a way I could improve? Is there something I learned that I could apply differently next time?"
- If you mix the two -- trying to self-correct while performing -- you will be paralysed by the crow epistemology. You cannot hold content *and* six methods simultaneously.

### Communication as Self-Assertion

The ideal result of learning these principles is that communicating ideas becomes an enjoyable experience -- an act of **self-assertion**, not a dutiful following of impossible rules.

## Practical Action Items

1. **When actually writing or speaking, forget every rule you have learned.** Just do it. Go with what you have. Afterwards, evaluate.

2. **Learn one principle at a time.** Do not try to master everything at once. Focus on one area (e.g., motivation, or self-containment) until it becomes second nature, then add another.

3. **Review after each performance.** What worked? What did not? What one thing would you change? Apply that insight next time.

4. **Do not aim for perfection.** There is no Platonic ideal of the perfect article or talk. There are many legitimate options within broad principles. Your goal is continuous improvement, not an impossible standard.

5. **Enjoy the process.** Communication is an act of self-expression and self-assertion. If it feels like drudgery, something is wrong with your approach, not with the activity.
